My research centers on environmental exposures—how air pollution, neighborhood environments, and climate-related hazards shape cancer risk, mortality, and survivorship, and why those burdens fall unevenly across communities. I quantify these exposures and their health impacts using geospatial and epidemiologic methods in large U.S. population studies.

Research

Research Areas

Air pollution & Health

A systematic review and meta-analysis of PM2.5 components and emission sources, spanning short- and long-term exposures as well as mortality and morbidity. The evidence is further shared through an online data platform with interactive visualizations.

PM2.5 Components Emission Sources Evidence Synthesis Data Extraction Meta-Analysis Interactive Visualization

Environmental Exposures & Cancer Risk

Examining how air pollution, greenness, and related environmental exposures shape cancer incidence, mortality, and survivorship in large prospective cohorts. Geospatial and spatiotemporal approaches support time-varying exposure assessment at fine spatial scales and help characterize how multiple exposures jointly shape cancer outcomes.

Geocoding & GIS-Based Linkage Spatiotemporal Modeling Remote Sensing-Based Exposure Assessment Cox PH Models

Climate Hazards & Infrastructure Resilience

Mapping spatial patterns of climate-related hazards, infrastructure systems, and social vulnerability across communities. Characterizing compounded risks and their implications for resilience and equity.

Hazard Mapping Critical Infrastructure Spatial Clustering Compounded Risk

Previous Works

Earlier research on chemical exposures, including endocrine-disrupting chemicals, heavy metals, and humidifier disinfectants, laid the foundation for my career in environmental health.

About

Background

I am a PhD candidate in Environmental Health Sciences at the University of Arizona's Mel & Enid Zuckerman College of Public Health, where I work in the Health & Urban Modeling Lab under Professor Chris C. Lim. I am expected to complete my PhD in 2027 and am on the market for postdoctoral positions beginning that year.

My research sits at the intersection of environmental epidemiology, geospatial analysis, and health equity. I study how the places people live — defined by air quality, access to green space, and the resilience of surrounding infrastructure — shape cancer outcomes and health disparities across the United States.

I hold a Master of Science in Humanities and Social Medicine from Gachon University (South Korea), where I studied phthalate exposures and physical function in Korean elderly populations, and a Bachelor of Engineering in Biomedical Engineering, also from Gachon University.

Before joining the University of Arizona, I worked as a researcher and field interviewer on the national investigation of Humidifier Disinfectant-Associated Lung Injury in South Korea — one of the largest environmental health incidents in Korean history. That work shaped my commitment to understanding how environmental exposures harm vulnerable populations.
